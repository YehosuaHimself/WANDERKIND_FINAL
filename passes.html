Bread · Salt · Welcome

A meal at the host's table, when one is offered.

The Food Pass invites — never claims. The bearer carries it as a sign that a place at the table is welcome when a host has one to give. It is a request, not an entitlement.

It exists because the oldest gesture of hospitality — bread and salt at the door — is also the smallest. Most kitchens have a chair to spare for a quiet stranger who walked twenty kilometres to reach them.

Class
A · request
Covenant
Article II · The Door
What it permits
  • A meal at the host's table when one is offered.
  • Morning bread, evening soup, leftovers in good faith.
  • The conversation that comes with the chair.
  • Nothing more — the host is always free to decline.
How it works
  • You walk up, the host sees the Pass on your phone.
  • They offer or they don't — kindly, either way.
  • No payment passes through Wanderkind. The vouch is the only currency.

Water · Shower · Rest

Water at the door — bottle, tap, shower. The smallest unit of welcome.

The Water Pass is the most modest of the three. It asks for the thing that no host who has the means ever refuses on a hot day or in a downpour: water. A refill, a tap, a five-minute shower, a sit-down in the shade.

It is what makes a long day possible. It is also what allows hospitality to begin without obligation — a glass of water leaves no debt and asks no question.

Class
A · request
Covenant
Article II · The Door
What it permits
  • A bottle refilled at the tap.
  • A shower after rain or twenty kilometres.
  • A bench in the shade for ten minutes.
  • A roof from a sudden storm.
How it works
  • You knock, present the Water Pass, the host offers what they can.
  • If nothing — no harm. The next door is on the trail.
  • If a refill or shower — thank them in the morning vouch.
